# The Quiet Migration to Local AI Inference

Over the past 18 months, a structural shift has been unfolding in how product teams ship AI features: more of them now run inference locally — on the user's device, or on small self-hosted GPU clusters — rather than calling a hosted frontier API for every request. Three forces are driving this shift, and together they explain why even teams that started with OpenAI or Anthropic SaaS APIs are now investing in local pipelines.

The first force is cost asymmetry. A typical conversational product makes between 5 and 50 model calls per session, depending on agentic depth. At hosted-API rates, a single power user generates $4-$15 per month in inference cost — small individually, ruinous at scale. Teams that successfully transitioned a portion of their workload to a 30B-parameter open-weight model running on consumer-grade Apple Silicon report inference cost reductions of 80-95%, with the only ongoing cost being device energy consumption. The catch is that not all workloads transition cleanly: complex reasoning, large-context retrieval, and tool-heavy agent loops still benefit from frontier-model quality. A common pattern is to route 70% of "easy" calls (classification, summarization, structured extraction) to a local model and reserve frontier APIs for the residual 30% of high-stakes work.

The second force is privacy regulation. The EU AI Act and similar frameworks in Asia-Pacific now require teams to perform documented data-flow analyses for any system that sends user content to a third party for processing. For products handling health data, financial records, or anything covered by sectoral privacy laws (HIPAA, GLBA), the simplest compliance posture is to never send user data outside the company's controlled boundary. Local inference — whether on-device for consumer apps, or on company-managed GPUs for enterprise — collapses this entire compliance surface. The competitive advantage is no longer "we use the best model"; it's "we can prove your data never left your premises."

The third force is engineering velocity. Hosted APIs introduce three latency variables that local inference removes entirely: network round-trip, queue depth at the provider, and per-request rate limits. For agentic workflows that loop 10-20 times per user interaction, a 200ms baseline per call quickly becomes a 2-4 second user-visible delay. Local inference on optimized hardware (e.g. Apple Silicon with MLX or NVIDIA workstations with vLLM) typically delivers first-token latency under 50ms and sustained throughput in the 30-100 tokens-per-second range for 30B-class models — fast enough that the agent loop feels interactive rather than batched.

The trade-off is real and worth naming: local 30B-parameter models are still measurably worse than frontier models at multi-step reasoning, novel domain transfer, and edge-of-distribution prompts. The smartest teams treat local-vs-hosted as a routing decision per request, not a one-time architectural choice. Build the routing layer first, instrument the failure modes (especially silent quality regressions), and migrate workloads one at a time as you validate each.

The teams winning this transition aren't the ones who picked the best model. They're the ones who built the discipline to know, on every request, which model is the right tool — and who measure outcomes well enough to keep that judgment honest.
